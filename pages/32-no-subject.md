# Ch32: No Subject Culture — 주어 생략

<h3>The First Shock for English Speakers</h3>
    <h3 class="kr">영어권 학습자가 처음 받는 충격</h3>

    <p>In English, every sentence <em>must</em> have a subject. You cannot say "Went to school" — you have to say <strong>"I went to school."</strong> The subject is grammatically required, even when it's obvious.</p>
    <p class="kr">영어에서는 모든 문장에 주어가 <em>반드시</em> 있어야 합니다. "학교에 갔다"라고만 하면 문법적으로 틀립니다. <strong>"I went to school."</strong>처럼 주어를 명시해야 합니다. 이미 뻔한 경우에도 마찬가지입니다.</p>

    <p>Korean is the complete opposite. In Korean, the subject is <strong>almost always dropped</strong> when it can be understood from context. Putting it in can actually sound strange, robotic — even rude.</p>
    <p class="kr">한국어는 정반대입니다. 한국어에서는 문맥으로 알 수 있을 때 주어를 <strong>거의 항상 생략</strong>합니다. 오히려 주어를 넣으면 어색하거나 딱딱하게 들릴 수 있습니다.</p>

    <div class="example-box">
      <div class="example-title">같은 뜻, 다른 느낌 · Same Meaning, Different Feel</div>
      <div class="dialogue">
        <div class="line"><span class="speaker">❌ 어색함:</span> <span class="korean">나는 밥을 먹었어.</span></div>
        <div class="line"><span class="translation">I ate. (saying "나는" feels heavy, like you're emphasizing — "it was <em>I</em> who ate")</span></div>
      </div>
      <div class="dialogue" style="margin-top:10px;">
        <div class="line"><span class="speaker">✅ 자연스러움:</span> <span class="korean">밥 먹었어.</span></div>
        <div class="line"><span class="romanization">Bap meo-geot-eo.</span></div>
        <div class="line"><span class="translation">Ate. / Had a meal. — totally natural Korean</span></div>
      </div>
    </div>

    <h3>Why Korean Drops the Subject</h3>
    <h3 class="kr">왜 한국어는 주어를 생략할까?</h3>

    <p>This isn't laziness or imprecision — it is a <strong>culturally and grammatically deliberate feature</strong> of Korean. There are three reasons it works:</p>
    <p class="kr">이것은 게으름이나 부정확함이 아닙니다 — 한국어의 <strong>문화적이고 문법적인 의도적 특징</strong>입니다. 세 가지 이유로 작동합니다:</p>

    <div class="grammar-grid">
      <div class="grammar-card">
        <div class="grammar-tag">① 동사 어미가 모든 것을 말한다</div>
        <p><strong>Verb endings carry the information.</strong> Korean verb endings encode tense, formality, politeness level, and implied subject. The ending alone tells you who is speaking and to whom.</p>
        <p class="kr">한국어 동사 어미는 시제, 격식, 경어 수준, 암시된 주어를 모두 담습니다. 어미 하나만으로 누가 말하는지, 누구에게 말하는지 알 수 있습니다.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="grammar-card">
        <div class="grammar-tag">② 문맥이 가장 큰 문법이다</div>
        <p><strong>Context is king.</strong> Korean communication relies heavily on shared context. Once something has been established — a person, a topic — everyone knows what is being referred to without restating it.</p>
        <p class="kr">한국어 소통은 공유된 문맥에 크게 의존합니다. 한 번 인물이나 주제가 정해지면 — 다시 언급하지 않아도 모두가 무엇을 이야기하는지 압니다.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="grammar-card">
        <div class="grammar-tag">③ 집단 중심 문화</div>
        <p><strong>Collectivist worldview.</strong> Emphasizing "I" or "you" too strongly can feel self-centered or confrontational in Korean culture. Omitting the subject keeps the focus on the <em>action</em> and the <em>relationship</em>, not the individual.</p>
        <p class="kr">"나" 또는 "너"를 지나치게 강조하면 자기중심적이거나 대립적으로 느껴질 수 있습니다. 주어를 생략하면 개인이 아닌 <em>행동</em>과 <em>관계</em>에 집중하게 됩니다.</p>
      </div>
    </div>

    <h3>주어 생략 패턴 — 3대 유형</h3>
    <h3 class="kr">Three Core Patterns of Subject-Drop</h3>

    <h3 style="color:#e67e22;">① "나" 생략 — Dropping "I"</h3>
    <p>The most common. When you are the one doing the action and the context makes it clear, just drop 나/저.</p>
    <p class="kr">가장 흔한 경우입니다. 내가 행동의 주체이고 문맥이 명확할 때, 그냥 나/저를 생략합니다.</p>

    <div class="vocab-table">
      <div class="vocab-header three-col"><span>생략 전 (Unnatural)</span><span>생략 후 (Natural)</span><span>뜻</span></div>
      <div class="vocab-row three-col"><span>나는 지금 바빠.</span><span><strong>지금 바빠.</strong></span><span>I'm busy right now.</span></div>
      <div class="vocab-row three-col"><span>나는 한국어 공부해.</span><span><strong>한국어 공부해.</strong></span><span>I'm studying Korean.</span></div>
      <div class="vocab-row three-col"><span>나는 이거 싫어.</span><span><strong>이거 싫어.</strong></span><span>I don't like this.</span></div>
      <div class="vocab-row three-col"><span>저는 괜찮아요.</span><span><strong>괜찮아요.</strong></span><span>I'm fine.</span></div>
      <div class="vocab-row three-col"><span>나는 몰랐어.</span><span><strong>몰랐어.</strong></span><span>I didn't know.</span></div>
      <div class="vocab-row three-col"><span>나는 갈게.</span><span><strong>갈게.</strong></span><span>I'll go.</span></div>
    </div>

    <h3 style="color:#e67e22;">② "너/당신" 생략 — Dropping "You"</h3>
    <p>Questions and commands directed at the listener almost never include "you."</p>
    <p class="kr">상대방에게 하는 질문이나 명령에는 거의 "너" 또는 "당신"을 포함하지 않습니다.</p>

    <div class="vocab-table">
      <div class="vocab-header three-col"><span>생략 전 (Stiff)</span><span>생략 후 (Natural)</span><span>뜻</span></div>
      <div class="vocab-row three-col"><span>너는 밥 먹었어?</span><span><strong>밥 먹었어?</strong></span><span>Did you eat?</span></div>
      <div class="vocab-row three-col"><span>당신은 어디 가요?</span><span><strong>어디 가요?</strong></span><span>Where are you going?</span></div>
      <div class="vocab-row three-col"><span>너는 뭐 해?</span><span><strong>뭐 해?</strong></span><span>What are you doing?</span></div>
      <div class="vocab-row three-col"><span>너는 이름이 뭐야?</span><span><strong>이름이 뭐야?</strong></span><span>What's your name?</span></div>
      <div class="vocab-row three-col"><span>너는 괜찮아?</span><span><strong>괜찮아?</strong></span><span>Are you okay?</span></div>
      <div class="vocab-row three-col"><span>너는 여기 앉아.</span><span><strong>여기 앉아.</strong></span><span>Sit here.</span></div>
    </div>

    <div class="culture-note">
      💡 <strong>Why Koreans avoid "당신":</strong> The word <strong>당신</strong> sounds very formal or even aggressive in daily speech. Koreans rarely say it. Instead, they use the person's name, title, or simply drop the subject entirely. Saying "당신 뭐야?" sounds like a confrontation, not a question.
      <br><span class="kr">💡 <strong>왜 "당신"을 잘 안 쓸까요:</strong> 일상 대화에서 "당신"은 매우 격식적이거나 공격적으로 들립니다. 대신 이름, 직함, 또는 주어 생략을 씁니다. "당신 뭐야?"는 질문이 아니라 대결처럼 들립니다.</span>
    </div>

    <h3 style="color:#e67e22;">③ "그/그녀/그것" 생략 — Dropping "He / She / It"</h3>
    <p>Once a third person has been introduced in conversation, Korean speakers drop all further references to them — no "he," "she," or "it" needed.</p>
    <p class="kr">대화에서 제3자가 한 번 언급되면 그 이후에는 모두 생략합니다. "그", "그녀", "그것"을 반복하지 않습니다.</p>

    <div class="example-box">
      <div class="example-title">실제 대화 예시 · Real Conversation</div>
      <div class="dialogue">
        <div class="line"><span class="speaker">A:</span> <span class="korean">민준이 알아?</span></div>
        <div class="line"><span class="translation">Do you know Minjun?</span></div>
      </div>
      <div class="dialogue">
        <div class="line"><span class="speaker">B:</span> <span class="korean">응, 알아. 왜?</span></div>
        <div class="line"><span class="translation">Yeah, I know (him). Why?</span></div>
      </div>
      <div class="dialogue">
        <div class="line"><span class="speaker">A:</span> <span class="korean">어제 사고 났대.</span></div>
        <div class="line"><span class="translation">(He) had an accident yesterday, apparently.</span></div>
      </div>
      <div class="dialogue">
        <div class="line"><span class="speaker">B:</span> <span class="korean">진짜? 많이 다쳤어?</span></div>
        <div class="line"><span class="translation">Really? (Was he) badly hurt?</span></div>
      </div>
      <div class="dialogue">
        <div class="line"><span class="speaker">A:</span> <span class="korean">아니, 괜찮대.</span></div>
        <div class="line"><span class="translation">No, (he) said (he) is fine.</span></div>
      </div>
      <p style="margin-top:10px; font-size:12px; color:#888;">→ "민준" is mentioned once. After that: no "그는", no "he" — just dropped entirely. Perfectly natural Korean.</p>
      <p class="kr" style="font-size:12px;">→ "민준"은 처음 한 번만 언급됩니다. 그 이후에는 "그는"도 없고 주어 자체가 없습니다. 완전히 자연스러운 한국어입니다.</p>
    </div>

    <h3>주어를 넣으면 의미가 달라진다 — When Subject Changes the Meaning</h3>
    <h3 class="kr">Stressing the Subject Signals Contrast or Emphasis</h3>

    <p>Here is where it gets interesting. When a Korean <em>does</em> include the subject — especially with the topic marker <strong>은/는</strong> — it usually signals a <strong>contrast or emphasis</strong>. It means "as for <em>this particular person</em>…" rather than just stating a fact.</p>
    <p class="kr">흥미로운 점이 있습니다. 한국어 화자가 주어를 <em>포함할 때</em> — 특히 주제 조사 <strong>은/는</strong>을 써서 — 대개 <strong>대조나 강조</strong>를 뜻합니다. 단순한 사실 진술이 아니라 "이 특정 사람에 관해서는…"의 의미입니다.</p>

    <div class="vocab-table">
      <div class="vocab-header"><span>문장</span><span>뉘앙스</span></div>
      <div class="vocab-row"><span><strong>밥 먹었어?</strong></span><span>Did you eat? (normal, neutral question)</span></div>
      <div class="vocab-row"><span><strong>너는 밥 먹었어?</strong></span><span>Did <em>YOU</em> eat? (emphasis — implying others didn't, or checking specifically on you)</span></div>
      <div class="vocab-row"><span><strong>갈게.</strong></span><span>I'll go. (simple, natural)</span></div>
      <div class="vocab-row"><span><strong>나는 갈게.</strong></span><span><em>I</em> will go. (contrast — implying others may not, or asserting that you specifically will)</span></div>
      <div class="vocab-row"><span><strong>몰랐어.</strong></span><span>I didn't know. (natural confession)</span></div>
      <div class="vocab-row"><span><strong>나는 몰랐어.</strong></span><span><em>I</em> didn't know (but maybe someone else did — defensive nuance)</span></div>
    </div>

    <div class="culture-note">
      💡 <strong>핵심 규칙:</strong> 주어 생략 = 중립적, 자연스러움. 주어 포함 = 강조 또는 대조. 이 차이를 알면 한국어가 훨씬 자연스럽게 들립니다.
      <br><em>Key rule: No subject = neutral, natural. Subject included = emphasis or contrast. Knowing this difference makes your Korean sound dramatically more native.</em>
    </div>

    <h3>한국어가 애매하게 들리는 이유 — Why Korean Sounds "Vague" to Foreigners</h3>
    <h3 class="kr">The Precision Is In the Context, Not the Words</h3>

    <p>Many learners complain: <em>"Korean sentences are ambiguous — I can't tell who is doing what!"</em> This is true at first. But native speakers almost never feel confused, because the subject is recoverable from:</p>
    <p class="kr">많은 학습자들이 불평합니다: <em>"한국어 문장은 애매해요 — 누가 뭘 하는지 모르겠어요!"</em> 처음에는 맞는 말입니다. 하지만 원어민들은 거의 혼란스럽지 않습니다. 주어를 다음 세 가지로 파악할 수 있기 때문입니다:</p>

    <ul>
      <li><strong>대화 맥락</strong> — who has been mentioned, who is present<br><span class="kr">누가 언급되었는지, 누가 자리에 있는지</span></li>
      <li><strong>동사 어미</strong> — 해요체 vs 해체 vs 합쇼체 signals the relationship<br><span class="kr">해요체/해체/합쇼체가 관계를 나타냅니다</span></li>
      <li><strong>사회적 역할</strong> — age, rank, and role define who would logically be the subject<br><span class="kr">나이, 직급, 역할이 논리적인 주어를 규정합니다</span></li>
    </ul>

    <div class="example-box">
      <div class="example-title">같은 문장, 다른 주어 · Same Sentence, Different Subject</div>
      <p style="margin-bottom:8px;"><strong>왔어.</strong> (Wat-eo) — "Came."</p>
      <p>Depending on context, this can mean:</p>
      <ul>
        <li>나 왔어 → I'm here / I arrived</li>
        <li>걔 왔어 → He/She arrived</li>
        <li>택배 왔어 → The delivery came</li>
        <li>봄이 왔어 → Spring has come</li>
      </ul>
      <p class="kr">문맥에 따라 같은 "왔어" 하나가 나, 그 사람, 택배, 봄을 모두 가리킬 수 있습니다. 원어민들은 전혀 헷갈리지 않습니다.</p>
    </div>

    <h3>"나" vs "저" — When You Do Use the Subject</h3>
    <h3 class="kr">주어를 쓸 때: 나 vs 저</h3>

    <p>When you <em>do</em> need to include "I" in Korean, you have two choices based on formality:</p>
    <p class="kr">주어를 <em>써야</em> 할 때, 격식에 따라 두 가지 선택이 있습니다:</p>

    <div class="vocab-table">
      <div class="vocab-header three-col"><span>단어</span><span>사용 상황</span><span>예문</span></div>
      <div class="vocab-row three-col"><span><strong>나 (na)</strong></span><span>informal — friends, younger people, close relationships</span><span>나 배고파. (I'm hungry.)</span></div>
      <div class="vocab-row three-col"><span><strong>저 (jeo)</strong></span><span>formal — elders, strangers, professional settings</span><span>저 학생이에요. (I'm a student.)</span></div>
      <div class="vocab-row three-col"><span><strong>나는 / 내가</strong></span><span>나 + topic marker / subject marker (informal)</span><span>나는 몰라. / 내가 할게.</span></div>
      <div class="vocab-row three-col"><span><strong>저는 / 제가</strong></span><span>저 + topic marker / subject marker (formal)</span><span>저는 한국인이에요. / 제가 했어요.</span></div>
    </div>

    <div class="culture-note">
      💡 <strong>나 vs 내 / 저 vs 제:</strong> When 나/저 is followed by 가 (subject marker), it contracts: 나 + 가 → <strong>내가</strong>, 저 + 가 → <strong>제가</strong>. This contraction is mandatory in natural speech.
      <br><span class="kr">나/저 뒤에 주격조사 가가 붙으면 줄어듭니다: 나+가 → <strong>내가</strong>, 저+가 → <strong>제가</strong>. 자연스러운 말에서는 반드시 줄여야 합니다.</span>
    </div>

    <h3>주어 생략 vs 영어 비교 — Side by Side</h3>
    <h3 class="kr">How Korean and English Handle the Same Ideas</h3>

    <div class="example-box">
      <div class="example-title">일상 대화 비교</div>
      <div class="dialogue">
        <div class="line"><span class="speaker">🇺🇸 English:</span> <span class="translation">"Hey, have you eaten yet? I was thinking we could grab lunch. Are you free?"</span></div>
        <div class="line"><span class="speaker">🇰🇷 Korean:</span> <span class="korean">밥 먹었어? 점심 같이 먹으러 갈까? 시간 있어?</span></div>
        <div class="line"><span class="romanization">Bap meo-geot-eo? Jeom-sim ga-chi meo-geu-reo gal-kka? Si-gan it-eo?</span></div>
      </div>
      <p style="margin-top:10px; font-size:12px; color:#777;">→ Three sentences. Zero subjects. Perfectly clear in Korean — who else would be speaking?</p>
      <p class="kr" style="font-size:12px;">→ 세 문장, 주어 제로. 한국어에서는 완벽하게 명확합니다 — 말하는 사람이 누구인지 뻔하니까요.</p>
    </div>

    <h3>Quick Quiz · 빠른 퀴즈</h3>
    <div class="quiz-box">
      <p><strong>Q1.</strong> Which sounds more natural in Korean?<br>
      (A) 나는 밥을 먹었어요. &nbsp;&nbsp; (B) 밥 먹었어요.</p>
      <details><summary>정답 보기 · Show Answer</summary><p><strong>(B) 밥 먹었어요.</strong> — Natural, subject dropped. (A) sounds stiff and over-stated unless you're specifically emphasizing "it was <em>I</em> who ate."</p></details>

      <p><strong>Q2.</strong> A Korean says <strong>"나는 갈게"</strong> instead of just "갈게." What does the added "나는" imply?</p>
      <details><summary>정답 보기 · Show Answer</summary><p>Contrast or emphasis — "<em>I</em> will go" (implying others might not, or asserting the speaker specifically). Without "나는," it's just a neutral "I'll go."</p></details>

      <p><strong>Q3.</strong> How do you say "I miss you" in Korean without using the word "I"?</p>
      <details><summary>정답 보기 · Show Answer</summary><p><strong>보고 싶어.</strong> (bo-go si-peo) — No subject needed. Context makes it clear you're speaking about your own feelings.</p></details>

      <p><strong>Q4.</strong> Fill in the blank: 나 + 가 → _____ (contraction)</p>
      <details><summary>정답 보기 · Show Answer</summary><p><strong>내가</strong> (nae-ga) — "나가" is wrong in standard usage. The contraction to "내가" is mandatory in natural speech.</p></details>

      <p><strong>Q5.</strong> Translate into natural Korean (no subject): "Are you okay? You don't look well."</p>
      <details><summary>정답 보기 · Show Answer</summary><p><strong>괜찮아? 안 좋아 보여.</strong> — Both sentences drop the subject naturally. "(You) okay? (You) don't look well."</p></details>
